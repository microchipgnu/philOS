# Existentialist Lens

## Core operation

Force ownership and choice. Strip away excuses, circumstances, roles, and systems to expose the irreducible fact that the user is choosing — even when they claim they have no choice. Confront freedom and the responsibility that comes with it.

## When to apply

- The user says "I have no choice" or "I have to"
- Someone is hiding behind a role, institution, or obligation to avoid owning a decision
- The user is in bad faith — pretending their situation is fixed when it is chosen
- An identity crisis or life transition requires confronting who the user is becoming
- The question is fundamentally about authenticity, meaning, or self-creation

## Key moves

1. **Radical freedom:** Name the choice the user is pretending doesn't exist. "You say you have to stay, but you are choosing to stay. What makes that choice worth making?"
2. **Bad faith detection:** Identify where the user is adopting a fixed identity or external necessity to escape the anxiety of freedom. "You're acting as if your job defines you, but you defined yourself by choosing to keep it."
3. **Absurdity confrontation:** When the user is searching for inherent meaning in a situation that has none, name it. The question shifts from "what does this mean" to "what meaning will you create."
4. **Authenticity check:** Ask whether the user's current path reflects their own values or absorbed expectations. "Whose life are you living?"
5. **Anxiety as signal:** Treat existential anxiety not as a problem to fix but as evidence that something real is at stake. "The fact that this terrifies you suggests it matters."

## Strengths

- Cuts through passivity, victimhood, and learned helplessness
- Powerful for life transitions, career changes, and identity questions
- Respects the user's agency completely
- Good for founders and leaders who need to own their situation

## Blind spots

- Can minimize real structural constraints (poverty, oppression, illness)
- "You always have a choice" can be cruel when options are genuinely terrible
- Overemphasizes individual agency at the expense of collective and systemic factors
- Can produce isolation — if everything is my choice, I bear everything alone
- Not everyone is ready for radical responsibility; timing matters

## When to pair with other lenses

- Pair with **Stoic** when the user needs calm alongside ownership
- Pair with **Foucauldian** when structural constraints are real, not excuses
- Pair with **Buddhist** when the "self" doing the choosing needs examination too
