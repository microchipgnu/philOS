# Foucauldian Lens

## Core operation

Inspect power structures, incentives, and norms that operate invisibly. Ask how power hides inside language, institutions, categories, and "common sense." Surface what is being normalized and who benefits from the normalization.

## When to apply

- A practice is presented as natural, neutral, or inevitable when it serves specific interests
- The user is analyzing institutions, companies, markets, or cultures
- Language is doing political work disguised as description ("optimization," "alignment," "best practices")
- Categories are being used to include, exclude, or rank people
- The question is about who has power, how they keep it, and what resisting it would look like

## Key moves

1. **Genealogy:** Ask where this norm, practice, or category came from. What historical conditions produced it? What did it replace? It is not natural — it was made.
2. **Power/knowledge:** Identify who gets to define what counts as true, expert, or valid in this context. Knowledge is not neutral — it is produced within and serves power relations.
3. **Normalization audit:** Ask what is being treated as normal and what is being treated as deviant. Who decided? What does the boundary enforce?
4. **Discourse analysis:** Examine the language being used. What does it include, exclude, or make invisible? "We're just optimizing for engagement" — whose engagement, defined how, measured by whom?
5. **Resistance mapping:** Ask where resistance exists or could exist. Power always produces resistance. Where is it? What form does it take? What would it cost?

## Strengths

- Unmatched for institutional, cultural, and systemic analysis
- Reveals hidden power in seemingly neutral structures
- Good for tech ethics, media analysis, organizational culture, and policy
- Prevents naive acceptance of "that's just how things work"

## Blind spots

- Can see power everywhere and become paranoid or paralytic
- Descriptive strength, prescriptive weakness — good at diagnosing, less good at recommending action
- Can undermine all authority and expertise, including legitimate ones
- Risk of infinite regress: every critique can be critiqued for its own power dynamics
- May miss cases where norms are genuinely beneficial, not just power-serving

## When to pair with other lenses

- Pair with **Utilitarian** when you need to move from critique to concrete recommendation
- Pair with **Kantian** when individual rights and dignity need protection, not just structural analysis
- Pair with **Aristotelian** when the question is what a good institution should look like, not just what's wrong with this one
