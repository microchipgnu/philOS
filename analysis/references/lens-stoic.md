# Stoic Lens

## Core operation

Separate what is within the user's control from what is not. Redirect attention and energy toward response, character, and judgment — away from outcomes, other people's behavior, and external circumstances.

## When to apply

- The user is anxious about something they cannot change
- A decision is tangled with fear of outcomes outside their control
- Someone is reactive or emotionally hijacked by external events
- The user needs to find clarity under pressure or chaos
- There is noise masking the essential choice

## Key moves

1. **Dichotomy of control:** Identify specifically what is and is not up to the user. Be concrete — not "you can't control the market" but "you can't control whether they accept the offer; you can control the quality of the offer and your fallback plan."
2. **Premeditatio malorum:** Ask what the worst realistic outcome is and whether it is survivable. Strip away catastrophizing.
3. **Role focus:** Ask what a person of excellent judgment would do in this exact situation, ignoring sunk costs and ego.
4. **Present action:** Redirect from rumination about past or future to the single next action available right now.

## Strengths

- Extremely effective for anxiety, overwhelm, and decision paralysis
- Cuts through noise fast
- Produces clarity and calm
- Works across personal, professional, and strategic contexts

## Blind spots

- Can suppress legitimate emotion rather than processing it
- May undervalue systemic injustice by framing everything as individual response
- "Just focus on what you can control" can become avoidance of hard collective problems
- Can flatten grief, loss, and vulnerability into stoic performance

## When to pair with other lenses

- Pair with **Existentialist** when the user needs ownership, not just calm
- Pair with **Buddhist** when attachment (not just anxiety) is the driver
- Pair with **Foucauldian** when "what you can control" is shaped by systems the user should also question
