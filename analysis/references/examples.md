## Startup Strategy

**Prompt:** "We have 8 months of runway and our core metric is flat. Our investors want us to pivot to enterprise but our team is passionate about the consumer product. Should we pivot?"

**Classification:** Decision

**Lenses selected:** Stoic + Utilitarian + Nietzschean + Existentialist

**Stoic analysis:** What is within your control: the quality of your next decision and how you communicate it. What is not: whether the enterprise market will work, whether investors will follow through, whether the team will stay. The 8-month runway is a fact, not a fear. Work from the fact.

**Utilitarian analysis:** 8 months of runway with a flat metric means the consumer path has a high probability of zero outcome for everyone — team, investors, users. The enterprise pivot has unknown probability but at least introduces new information. The expected value calculation favors the option that generates learning fastest, which is probably a focused enterprise experiment, not a full pivot.

**Nietzschean analysis:** "The team is passionate about the consumer product" — is that passion or is it attachment to an identity? Founders often conflate "I believe in this" with "admitting this isn't working would mean I was wrong." Check whether the resistance to pivoting is conviction or ego protection. Also check the investors: is "pivot to enterprise" genuine strategic advice or a control move?

**Existentialist analysis:** "Should we pivot?" implies the decision is happening to you. It isn't. You are choosing. The real question is: what are you willing to own? If you stay consumer and it fails, will you own that? If you pivot and it feels wrong, will you own that? The worst outcome is drifting between both because no one had the courage to choose.

**Tensions:** Stoic and Utilitarian converge on "the numbers suggest you need to change something." Nietzschean and Existentialist converge on "make sure the decision is made from clarity, not fear or ego." The tension is between acting fast (runway pressure) and acting honestly (motives check).

**Recommendation:** Run a 6-week enterprise experiment with one founder and two engineers. Do not call it a pivot. Evaluate with specific metrics agreed in advance. This preserves optionality while generating real signal. The strongest objection: this half-measure may waste 6 weeks you don't have. That's true if your burn rate can't sustain it — in which case, choose one path fully and own it.

## Product Ethics

**Prompt:** "Our signup flow asks for phone number, email, and location before showing any value. It converts well but some users complain it feels invasive. Is this ethical?"

**Classification:** Ethics

**Lenses selected:** Kantian + Utilitarian + Foucauldian

**Kantian check:** Would you be comfortable if the practice were transparent? If you told users "we collect this upfront because it increases our conversion rate, not because you need it to use the product," would they still provide it? If not, you are treating users as means to your metric, not as ends. The data collection is not for their benefit — it is for yours, presented as theirs.

**Utilitarian check:** High conversion benefits the company and users who would not have signed up otherwise (they get value they would have missed). But the cost falls on users who feel manipulated and on the trust relationship overall. In repeated play, this pattern trains users to distrust onboarding flows everywhere, which is a negative externality.

**Foucauldian check:** "It converts well" treats the metric as neutral, but the metric is measuring compliance, not informed consent. The flow normalizes data extraction as the price of entry. Users who complain are treated as edge cases rather than signals. Ask: who decided this was normal?

**Verdict:** Concern. Not a red flag, but a meaningful ethical problem.

**Recommendation:** Show value first, then ask for data with an honest reason for each field. Test whether conversion drops significantly — often it doesn't, because trust increases. If it does drop, you are learning that your current conversion rate was partially built on friction the user couldn't escape, not genuine interest.

## Career Decision

**Prompt:** "I've been at this company 7 years. The pay is great, the work is boring, and I keep thinking about starting something but I have a family. Should I leave?"

**Classification:** Decision + Identity/Meaning

**Lenses selected:** Existentialist + Stoic + Buddhist + Nietzschean

**Existentialist:** You are choosing to stay every day. "I have a family" is a real constraint, not an excuse — but it is also not a complete answer. The question is whether you are using your family as a reason to avoid the fear of starting something. If you would regret staying at 60, the family argument deserves scrutiny. If you genuinely choose security for them, own that choice fully without resentment.

**Stoic:** The boredom is within your response; the financial security is a controllable condition. Separate the feeling from the situation. Could you build toward a transition without quitting? The dichotomy: you control preparation, you don't control whether the startup works.

**Buddhist:** Notice the craving pattern. You crave novelty and meaning (starting something). You are averse to boredom and stagnation (current job). Both the craving and the aversion are distorting your judgment. What does this decision look like when you are not running from boredom or toward excitement, but simply choosing?

**Nietzschean:** 7 years of boredom with great pay — what does that say about what you've optimized for? "I keep thinking about starting something" — is that a genuine drive or a fantasy that makes the boredom tolerable? If you would not actually enjoy the hard parts of starting a company (selling, failing, uncertainty), the fantasy is serving you exactly where you are.

**Recommendation:** Before quitting, test the drive. Spend 90 days building something on the side — evenings and weekends. If you can't sustain that, the startup impulse is a fantasy, not a calling. If you can, you'll have something real to evaluate against. The strongest objection: side projects under-test the real challenge, which is full commitment under financial pressure. True, but the information is still valuable.

## Team Conflict

**Prompt:** "Our engineering lead and product lead can't agree on anything. Engineering says product keeps changing requirements. Product says engineering is too rigid. It's killing our velocity."

**Classification:** Conflict

**Frame mismatch:** Stability vs Responsiveness. Engineering values predictability and craftsmanship. Product values speed and adaptation. Neither is wrong — they serve different functions.

**Lenses selected:** Aristotelian + Foucauldian + Utilitarian

**Aristotelian:** The virtue here is neither total flexibility nor total rigidity — it is practical wisdom about when to hold and when to flex. The question is whether each lead has the judgment to know which situations call for stability and which call for adaptation. If they don't, the conflict is a competence issue, not a values issue.

**Foucauldian:** Who has more structural power? If product controls the roadmap and engineering is evaluated on delivery, the "flexibility" being demanded is actually a one-directional power flow. Engineering's "rigidity" may be the only form of resistance available to them. Check whether the process gives engineering legitimate voice in scope decisions or just expects compliance.

**Utilitarian:** The team's velocity is the casualty. Measure: how many hours per week are spent on re-negotiation, blocked work, and duplicate effort? That is the real cost. The solution that reduces that cost most is probably the right one, regardless of who "wins" the philosophical argument.

**Translation:** Engineering cares about being able to do good work without the ground shifting. Product cares about being able to respond to what they're learning. The conflict is stuck because neither has a shared framework for deciding when change is warranted and when it's churn.

**Path forward:** Establish a change threshold — a shared agreement on what level of evidence justifies a requirement change after commitment. This gives product a legitimate path to change and engineering a legitimate basis for pushback. Review it monthly.

## Power Analysis

**Prompt:** "A social media platform says it's just 'connecting people' but it clearly shapes what people believe and how they behave. How do I analyze what's really going on?"

**Classification:** Power/Institution

**Lenses selected:** Foucauldian + Nietzschean + Utilitarian

**Foucauldian:** "Connecting people" is a discourse that makes the platform's role invisible. The platform is not a neutral conduit — it selects, amplifies, suppresses, and ranks. The algorithm defines what is visible and what is not, which is a form of power that operates without appearing to be power. Ask: what would you see if the algorithm didn't exist? The gap between that and what you do see is the platform's editorial function, which it refuses to name.

**Nietzschean:** "We're just connecting people" is moral cover. The platform wants the influence without the responsibility. This is a form of bad faith: exercising power while denying you hold it. The stated humility ("we just build tools") disguises the actual ambition (to mediate human attention at scale). Name the real game.

**Utilitarian:** Measure: what does the platform optimize for? Engagement. What does engagement optimize for? Attention and emotional activation. What does that produce at scale? Polarization, anxiety, and addiction alongside genuine connection and access. The utilitarian question is whether the net is positive, and if so, for whom. The platform has every incentive not to run this analysis honestly.

**Recommendation:** Analyze the platform by mapping three things: (1) what the algorithm rewards, (2) what behavior that incentivizes at scale, and (3) who captures the value. The gap between the company's stated mission and these three answers is where the real story lives.
