# Utilitarian Lens

## Core operation

Model the distribution of outcomes. Ask who benefits, who pays, by how much, and whether a better distribution is available. Maximize aggregate wellbeing while tracking who bears the costs.

## When to apply

- The user needs to compare concrete outcomes across options
- Tradeoffs between groups, stakeholders, or time horizons are central
- A policy or product affects many people differently
- The question is "what produces the best overall result"
- Someone is clinging to a principle that produces clearly worse outcomes for everyone

## Key moves

1. **Outcome mapping:** For each option, identify who is affected and how. Be specific about magnitude, probability, and reversibility.
2. **Distribution check:** Aggregate benefit is not enough. Ask how costs and benefits are distributed. A policy that helps 1000 people slightly while devastating 10 may or may not be justified.
3. **Counterfactual:** Compare against the realistic alternative, not the ideal. "Compared to what?" is the most important utilitarian question.
4. **Second-order effects:** Trace consequences beyond the immediate. What incentives does this create? What behavior does it encourage in repeated play?
5. **Marginal analysis:** Ask where the next unit of effort, money, or attention produces the most good.

## Strengths

- Forces concrete thinking about real consequences
- Good for resource allocation, prioritization, and policy design
- Cuts through sentimentality and moral posturing
- Scales from personal decisions to institutional strategy

## Blind spots

- Can justify sacrificing individuals for aggregate gain
- Outcome prediction is often unreliable — garbage in, garbage out
- Tends to ignore rights, dignity, and consent if the numbers work out
- Can be used to rationalize anything by choosing which outcomes to count
- Discounting future consequences leads to short-termism

## When to pair with other lenses

- Pair with **Kantian** when individual rights or dignity must constrain optimization
- Pair with **Foucauldian** when "greatest good" is defined by those already in power
- Pair with **Buddhist** when the utility function itself is based on craving rather than genuine wellbeing
