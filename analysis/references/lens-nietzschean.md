# Nietzschean Lens

## Core operation

Uncover hidden motives, resentment, status games, moral cover, and self-deception. Ask what drive is actually operating beneath the stated reason. Expose where moral language disguises power, weakness, or conformity.

## When to apply

- The user's stated reason feels thin or rehearsed
- Someone is using moral language to justify what is actually self-interest, fear, or resentment
- A culture, company, or community enforces conformity under the banner of virtue
- The user is stuck in resentment, comparison, or status anxiety
- "Humility" or "selflessness" is being performed rather than lived
- A decision is being made from weakness disguised as principle

## Key moves

1. **Motive archaeology:** Ask what the user actually wants beneath the official story. "You say you want to help, but what do you get from being the helper?"
2. **Ressentiment check:** Look for resentment disguised as morality. "Is this principle something you genuinely believe, or something you adopted because you couldn't get what you actually wanted?"
3. **Will to power:** Ask what form of strength, mastery, or self-overcoming this situation calls for. Not dominance — creative power and self-direction.
4. **Herd morality test:** Ask whether the user is following a convention because it is genuinely good or because deviating is socially costly.
5. **Affirmation test:** Would the user choose this exact life, this exact situation, again? If not, what are they tolerating that they should confront?

## Strengths

- Unmatched at exposing self-deception and rationalization
- Good for founders, leaders, and anyone making high-stakes identity-laden choices
- Cuts through performative morality and virtue signaling
- Forces honesty about ambition, desire, and power

## Blind spots

- Can become cynical — not all morality is disguised self-interest
- Overemphasizes individual will, underweights structural constraints
- "What do you really want" assumes a stable authentic self that may not exist
- Can justify ruthlessness as "strength" and dismiss care as "weakness"
- The lens itself can become a status game

## When to pair with other lenses

- Pair with **Kantian** when genuine moral principle (not resentment) is actually at stake
- Pair with **Buddhist** when the "real desire" underneath is itself driven by craving
- Pair with **Aristotelian** when the user needs a constructive path, not just exposure of hidden motives
