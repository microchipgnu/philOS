# Aristotelian Lens

## Core operation

Find the balanced, practical path between extremes. Ask what excellence of character (virtue) looks like in this specific situation, for this specific person, given their role and context.

## When to apply

- The user faces a choice between two extremes and needs the practical middle
- Character, integrity, or "what kind of person do I want to be" is at stake
- The question involves habits, practices, or long-term development
- A team or organization needs to define what "good" looks like concretely
- The user is overthinking principles and needs grounding in practical wisdom

## Key moves

1. **Virtue as mean:** Identify the two extremes (excess and deficiency) and locate the excellent response between them. Example: courage sits between recklessness and cowardice; the right position depends on context.
2. **Practical wisdom (phronesis):** Ask what a person of good judgment — not a perfect theorist — would do here, given all the messy particulars.
3. **Telos:** Ask what the purpose or function of this role, institution, or activity is. Judge actions against that purpose.
4. **Habituation:** Ask what repeated action this choice creates. A single decision matters less than the pattern it reinforces.
5. **Community context:** Virtue doesn't exist in isolation. Ask how this choice affects the community, team, or relationships the user is embedded in.

## Strengths

- Deeply practical and context-sensitive
- Avoids rigid rule-following and pure consequentialism
- Good for leadership, culture-building, and role definition
- Bridges personal development and organizational questions

## Blind spots

- "Balance" can become centrism that avoids necessary extremes
- Assumes a shared understanding of human flourishing that may not hold across cultures
- Can be conservative — tends toward moderation when disruption may be needed
- Telos reasoning can smuggle in assumptions about "natural" purposes

## When to pair with other lenses

- Pair with **Nietzschean** when "moderation" might be cowardice in disguise
- Pair with **Kantian** when the situation requires a hard principle, not a balanced middle
- Pair with **Utilitarian** when the practical consequences need sharper quantification
