# Kantian Lens

## Core operation

Test whether the principle behind an action is coherent and universalizable. Ask whether people are being treated as ends in themselves or merely as means. Check for consistency between what is said and what is done.

## When to apply

- The user is justifying an exception for themselves that they wouldn't grant others
- A policy, rule, or practice needs consistency testing
- Someone may be treating people instrumentally — as tools for their goals
- There is a gap between stated principles and actual behavior
- Rights, dignity, or consent are at stake

## Key moves

1. **Universalizability:** Extract the implicit rule behind the action. Ask: "If everyone in this situation followed this rule, would it still work?" If the rule self-destructs when universalized (e.g., "lie when convenient" destroys trust that makes lying effective), it fails.
2. **Humanity formula:** Ask whether the action treats any person merely as a means — as a tool, resource, or instrument — rather than also as an end with their own goals and dignity.
3. **Principle extraction:** Make the implicit maxim explicit. People rarely state their actual operating principle. Surface it: "The rule you're actually following is..."
4. **Consistency audit:** Compare stated values with actual behavior. Where do they diverge?

## Strengths

- Powerful for testing fairness and consistency
- Cuts through self-serving rationalizations
- Excellent for policy design, governance, and institutional rules
- Protects individual dignity against aggregate optimization

## Blind spots

- Can be rigid — sometimes good outcomes require rule-breaking
- Universalizability tests can produce absurd results at edge cases
- "Never treat people as means" is impossible in literal terms; the question is "merely" as means
- May miss context where different situations genuinely require different rules
- Can produce moral paralysis when principles conflict

## When to pair with other lenses

- Pair with **Utilitarian** when consequences matter more than principle consistency
- Pair with **Aristotelian** when practical wisdom should temper rigid rules
- Pair with **Nietzschean** when the stated principle may itself be a form of moral cover
